# Business and Management Theories Used in the Case Studies

## Customer-Based Brand Equity

Customer-based brand equity explains how brand knowledge, associations, loyalty, and trust create value beyond the functional product itself. A strong brand can support premium pricing, repeat purchase, resilience during mistakes, and emotional attachment, but it can also make consumers more resistant when managers violate the brand's meaning.

Common business examples include Coca-Cola customers rejecting New Coke because the original formula carried emotional and cultural meaning, Apple customers paying a premium for ecosystem trust, and luxury firms protecting scarcity to preserve brand value.

Reference: Keller, K. L. (1993). *Conceptualizing, measuring, and managing customer-based brand equity*. <https://doi.org/10.1177/002224299305700101>

## Brand Extension Theory

Brand extension theory studies when a company can use an existing brand name to enter a new product category. Extensions work best when customers see a clear fit between the parent brand and the new offering; they can fail when the extension confuses positioning or weakens the original brand.

Common business examples include LEGO extending into films and games while protecting the core brick identity, Coca-Cola introducing new beverage variants, and Virgin using its brand across travel, media, and financial services with mixed results depending on perceived fit.

Reference: Aaker, D. A., & Keller, K. L. (1990). *Consumer evaluations of brand extensions*. <https://doi.org/10.1177/002224299005400102>

## Defensive Marketing Strategy

Defensive marketing strategy explains how incumbents respond to competitive attacks through positioning, product changes, pricing, promotion, or channel moves. The goal is not only to win new customers but to protect the existing customer base and reduce switching.

Common business examples include Coca-Cola responding to Pepsi's taste-test challenge, telecom firms offering retention discounts when customers threaten to leave, and streaming platforms bundling services to reduce churn.

Reference: Hauser, J. R., & Shugan, S. M. (1983). *Defensive marketing strategies*. <https://doi.org/10.1287/mksc.2.4.319>

## Disruptive Innovation

Disruptive innovation explains how entrants can start with simpler, cheaper, or more convenient offerings for overlooked customers, then improve until they threaten incumbents. Incumbents often underreact because the early market looks too small, low-margin, or inferior compared with their core business.

Common business examples include Netflix challenging Blockbuster, digital photography undermining Kodak's film business, and cloud software replacing installed enterprise software.

Reference: Christensen, C. M., Raynor, M. E., & McDonald, R. (2015). *What is disruptive innovation?* Harvard Business Review. <https://hbr.org/2015/12/what-is-disruptive-innovation>

## Architectural Innovation

Architectural innovation occurs when the components of a product or system may remain familiar, but the way they are linked changes. Established firms can miss this kind of innovation because their knowledge, teams, and processes are organized around the old architecture.

Common business examples include digital cameras changing the photography system around sensors, storage, software, and sharing; streaming changing the video-rental architecture from stores and inventory to subscriptions and recommendation systems; and smartphones combining hardware, software, apps, and services in a new system design.

Reference: Henderson, R. M., & Clark, K. B. (1990). *Architectural innovation: The reconfiguration of existing product technologies and the failure of established firms*. <https://doi.org/10.2307/2393549>

## Business Model Innovation

Business model innovation focuses on changing how a firm creates, delivers, and captures value. The core issue is often not a new product alone, but a new revenue model, cost structure, customer relationship, channel, or ecosystem logic.

Common business examples include Adobe shifting from perpetual software licenses to Creative Cloud subscriptions, Netflix moving from DVD rental to streaming subscriptions, and software firms adopting freemium or usage-based pricing.

Reference: Johnson, M. W., Christensen, C. M., & Kagermann, H. (2008). *Reinventing your business model*. Harvard Business Review. <https://hbr.org/2008/12/reinventing-your-business-model>

## Two-Sided Markets

Two-sided market theory explains platforms that create value by connecting two or more interdependent groups, such as buyers and sellers, riders and drivers, or app developers and users. Pricing and governance decisions must consider cross-side effects, because a change for one group can affect the participation of another.

Common business examples include Apple balancing App Store users and developers, Uber balancing riders and drivers, Airbnb balancing guests and hosts, and credit-card networks balancing merchants and cardholders.

Reference: Rochet, J.-C., & Tirole, J. (2003). *Platform competition in two-sided markets*. <https://doi.org/10.1162/154247603322493212>

## Platform Governance and Network Effects

Platform governance covers the rules, access decisions, ranking systems, fees, enforcement mechanisms, and trust systems that shape platform behavior. Network effects make governance especially important because user value often rises or falls with participation, quality, and trust on the other side of the platform.

Common business examples include Twitter/X changing verification and moderation rules, Airbnb requiring clearer pricing displays, Apple controlling payment links and app review, and Uber using rating systems to coordinate marketplace quality.

Reference: Parker, G., Van Alstyne, M., & Choudary, S. P. (2016). *Platform revolution*. <https://wwnorton.com/books/Platform-Revolution/>

## Perceived Price Fairness

Perceived price fairness explains why customers may reject profitable pricing decisions when prices feel exploitative, hidden, arbitrary, or inconsistent with social norms. The issue is not only willingness to pay; it is whether customers believe the firm is respecting an implicit entitlement or relationship.

Common business examples include Airbnb customers reacting to hidden cleaning fees, Netflix users resisting password-sharing enforcement, Uber riders objecting to surge pricing during emergencies, and software customers objecting to forced subscription conversions.

Reference: Kahneman, D., Knetsch, J. L., & Thaler, R. H. (1986). *Fairness as a constraint on profit seeking: Entitlements in the market*. <https://www.jstor.org/stable/1806070>

## Strategic Pricing and Value Capture

Strategic pricing treats price as a management decision about value, segmentation, competitive positioning, and customer behavior. It goes beyond cost-plus pricing by asking how much value is created, who receives it, and how the firm can capture a fair share without damaging demand or trust.

Common business examples include Adobe converting software customers to subscriptions, Netflix pricing account sharing and ad-supported tiers, Uber using dynamic pricing to balance supply and demand, and SaaS firms using tiered plans.

Reference: Nagle, T. T., Muller, G., & Gruyaert, E. (2024). *The strategy and tactics of pricing*. <https://www.routledge.com/The-Strategy-and-Tactics-of-Pricing/Nagle-Muller-Gruyaert/p/book/9781032016828>

## Dynamic Capabilities

Dynamic capabilities theory explains how firms adapt by sensing changes, seizing opportunities, and reconfiguring resources as markets, technologies, and business models shift. It is especially useful when old strengths become sources of rigidity.

Common business examples include Microsoft shifting toward cloud and cross-platform services, Adobe building subscription and cloud capabilities, Meta reallocating resources toward new computing platforms, and Peloton adjusting operations after pandemic demand changed.

Reference: Teece, D. J., Pisano, G., & Shuen, A. (1997). *Dynamic capabilities and strategic management*. <https://www.jstor.org/stable/3088148>

## Innovation Management

Innovation management examines how organizations generate, select, fund, govern, and implement new ideas. The central tension is balancing experimentation and speed with discipline, accountability, and integration into the wider organization.

Common business examples include Samsung managing rapid product development while maintaining safety controls, LEGO narrowing innovation after overextension, Adobe managing a subscription transition, and Meta funding long-horizon R&D.

Reference: Van de Ven, A. H. (1986). *Central problems in the management of innovation*. <https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.32.5.590>

## Agency Theory

Agency theory examines conflicts that arise when one party delegates work or control to another, especially when incentives and information are misaligned. It helps explain why managers, founders, boards, investors, and employees may make decisions that do not maximize the interests of owners or stakeholders.

Common business examples include FTX customer funds being controlled without sufficient oversight, WeWork's founder-related governance issues, Theranos investors relying on incomplete information, and Volkswagen managers facing incentives that encouraged misconduct.

Reference: Jensen, M. C., & Meckling, W. H. (1976). *Theory of the firm: Managerial behavior, agency costs and ownership structure*. <https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-405X%2876%2990026-X>

## Corporate Governance and Separation of Ownership and Control

Corporate governance concerns the structures that direct and monitor organizations: boards, voting rights, audits, disclosure, control rights, and accountability systems. Separation of ownership and control explains why governance is needed when those who manage the firm are not the same as those who bear the full consequences.

Common business examples include board oversight failures at Theranos, founder control questions at Meta and WeWork, OpenAI's unusual nonprofit and capped-profit governance conflict, and Boeing's oversight problems around the 737 MAX.

Reference: Fama, E. F., & Jensen, M. C. (1983). *Separation of ownership and control*. <https://doi.org/10.1086/467037>

## Stakeholder Theory

Stakeholder theory argues that managers should consider the interests of groups that can affect or are affected by the firm, not only shareholders. It is often used to evaluate decisions involving employees, customers, regulators, communities, suppliers, and public trust.

Common business examples include Costco investing in employees while maintaining operational efficiency, Boeing balancing shareholder pressure with passenger safety, Volkswagen managing environmental harm and regulatory trust, and OpenAI balancing investors, employees, users, and public-interest claims.

Reference: Freeman, R. E. (1984). *Strategic management: A stakeholder approach*. <https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9781139192675>

## Situational Crisis Communication Theory

Situational Crisis Communication Theory links crisis response strategy to the organization's perceived responsibility for the crisis. The greater the responsibility or preventability, the more the firm needs accountability, corrective action, and rebuilding efforts rather than denial or minimization.

Common business examples include Samsung explaining the Galaxy Note7 battery failures, Boeing responding to the 737 MAX crashes, Volkswagen addressing Dieselgate, and platforms responding to safety or trust failures.

Reference: Coombs, W. T. (2007). *Protecting organization reputations during a crisis: The development and application of situational crisis communication theory*. <https://doi.org/10.1057/palgrave.crr.1550049>

## Swiss Cheese Model of Organizational Accidents

The Swiss Cheese Model explains accidents as the result of multiple layers of defense failing at the same time. Individual errors matter, but serious failures often reflect deeper weaknesses in design, process, incentives, communication, and oversight.

Common business examples include Boeing's 737 MAX certification and design failures, Samsung's Note7 battery and quality-control breakdowns, hospital safety incidents, and manufacturing defects that pass through several review stages.

Reference: Reason, J. (1990). *The contribution of latent human failures to the breakdown of complex systems*. <https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.1990.0090>

## Organizational Culture Theory

Organizational culture theory explains how shared assumptions, values, norms, rituals, and leadership behavior shape what people notice, reward, ignore, and punish. Culture is not only morale; it affects strategy execution, ethics, learning, and risk-taking.

Common business examples include Microsoft's shift from internal rivalry toward a learning culture, Twitter/X's cultural disruption after acquisition, Costco's employee-oriented operating culture, and startup cultures that normalize excessive secrecy or growth pressure.

Reference: Schein, E. H. (2010). *Organizational culture and leadership*. <https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Organizational+Culture+and+Leadership%2C+5th+Edition-p-9781119212041>

## Psychological Safety

Psychological safety is a team climate in which people believe they can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, and challenge decisions without interpersonal punishment. It matters because organizations need early warnings and honest learning before problems become crises.

Common business examples include engineers raising safety concerns, employees challenging unrealistic sales or product claims, Microsoft encouraging learning-oriented behavior, and boards creating channels for dissent before governance failures escalate.

Reference: Edmondson, A. C. (1999). *Psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams*. <https://doi.org/10.2307/2666999>

## Knowledge-Based View, Coordination, and Organizational Identity

The knowledge-based view sees firms as social systems for creating, coordinating, and applying knowledge. Identity matters because it helps employees coordinate around what the firm is uniquely good at and what it should stop doing.

Common business examples include LEGO refocusing around the brick and disciplined innovation, consulting firms organizing specialized expertise, and technology firms aligning product teams around shared platform knowledge.

Reference: Kogut, B., & Zander, U. (1996). *What firms do? Coordination, identity, and learning*. <https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.7.5.502>

## Brand Community

Brand community theory explains how consumers can form social relationships around a brand, including shared rituals, identity, knowledge, and loyalty. These communities can become strategic assets when firms listen to them and respect their role in product meaning.

Common business examples include LEGO adult fan communities contributing ideas and legitimacy, Apple developer and user communities reinforcing ecosystem loyalty, Harley-Davidson rider communities, and gaming communities influencing platform decisions.

Reference: Muniz, A. M., Jr., & O'Guinn, T. C. (2001). *Brand community*. <https://doi.org/10.1086/319618>

## Good Jobs Strategy

The Good Jobs Strategy argues that investing in employees can improve operations, customer service, productivity, and profitability when paired with disciplined process design. It challenges the assumption that low labor cost is always the path to efficiency.

Common business examples include Costco paying above many retail peers while maintaining strong productivity, retailers reducing hidden costs from turnover and poor service, and service firms designing simpler operations so employees can perform well.

Reference: Ton, Z. (2014). *The good jobs strategy*. <https://goodjobsinstitute.org/good-jobs-strategy/>

## Supply Chain Fit

Supply chain fit theory argues that supply chains should match product demand characteristics. Functional, predictable products require efficient supply chains, while innovative or uncertain products require responsive supply chains that can handle volatility.

Common business examples include Peloton misreading pandemic demand as durable demand, fashion retailers using responsive supply chains for uncertain trends, grocery firms emphasizing efficiency for stable staples, and electronics firms balancing speed with quality risk.

Reference: Fisher, M. L. (1997). *What is the right supply chain for your product?* Harvard Business Review. <https://hbr.org/1997/03/what-is-the-right-supply-chain-for-your-product>

## Greenwashing and Environmental Ethics

Greenwashing theory examines misleading environmental claims, symbols, omissions, or communications that create a false impression of sustainability. It is both a marketing ethics issue and a governance issue because misleading claims can damage stakeholder trust and trigger regulatory penalties.

Common business examples include Volkswagen's Dieselgate emissions claims, consumer-products companies exaggerating recyclability, airlines promoting carbon offsets without clear evidence, and energy firms selectively disclosing environmental performance.

Reference: Siano, A., Vollero, A., Conte, F., & Amabile, S. (2017). *More than words: Expanding the taxonomy of greenwashing after the Volkswagen scandal*. <https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbusres.2016.11.002>
